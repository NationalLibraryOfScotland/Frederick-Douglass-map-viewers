3. Frederick Douglass in Scotland

Scotland is at the heart of Frederick Douglass’s journey from slavery to freedom. Working tirelessly as “Scotland’s Anti-slavery agent,” he delivered hundreds of public lectures during his antislavery tours in 1846-and 1847 and again in 1859 and 1860. His impassioned lectures and powerful oratory attracted standing-room only crowds and gained him national renown. Due to popular demand, he repeatedly gave speeches not only in the major cities of Edinburgh, Glasgow and Aberdeen but in smaller cities, town and villages including, Perth, Dundee, Abroath, Montrose, Paisley, Ayr, Kilmarnock, Greenock, Bonhill, Hawick, Galashiels, Coldstream, Kelso, Falkirk, Dalkeith, Montrose, and Kirkcaldy. While for some cities, towns and villages research is still ongoing to identify the specific places where he gave speeches, here on this interactive map we have provided you with information about the lectures he delivered in locations for which we have full and verified details. Dedicating a lifetime to the fight against tyranny and oppression in all forms – whether it be the Highland Clearances in Scotland or chattel slavery in the United States – Douglass endorsed the following creed: “Right is of no sex, truth is of no color – God is the Father of Us All, and All We are Brethren.

  • This application forms part of the Our Bondage and Our Freedom project, which celebrates the 200 year anniversary of the birth of African American activist and author, Frederick Douglass.
  • It supports the Strike For Freedom: Slavery, Civil War and the Frederick Douglass Family in the Walter O Evans Collection treasures display (Thursday 4 October 2018 to Saturday 16 February 2019) in the National Library of Scotland, George IV Bridge, curated by Dora Petherbridge, Sarah Wilmott and Professor Celeste-Marie Bernier with Dr. Andrew Taylor.
  • It also forms part of the Our Bondage & Our Freedom: Frederick Douglass in Edinburgh project.
  • The content for this map has principally been gathered by Professor Celeste Marie-Bernier, with assistance from Nick Batho and Dr. Andrew Taylor.
